Background
Machine learning algorithms require huge amounts of data to train their models, but
data is dispersed across several organisations and individuals under the protection
of privacy regulations such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) [1].
These isolated datasets are individually too small or unrepresentative to train high-
quality models, but cannot simply be pooled due to legal and policy constraints.
Such phenomena are widespread across healthcare, finance, government, and supply
chains: a single hospital, for instance, may lack sufficient patient data to train a
reliable diagnostic model, yet sharing records with other hospitals is prohibited.
Federated Learning (FL) is a solution which allows the nodes in a distributed net-
work to train a shared global model by having each node train on its own local data
and transmit model gradients to a central server for aggregation, without sharing
the raw data. However, transmitting gradients introduces its own privacy risks. FL
has attracted significant research effort across a wide range of model types, includ-
ing deep neural networks, gradient boosted decision trees, and logistic regression,
and a corresponding range of privacy-preserving techniques (PPTs) have been pro-
posed to secure these systems, such as homomorphic encryption (HE), differential
privacy (DP) and secure multi-party computation (SMC) [2]. Lee et al. [2] proposed
a scheme that combined server-initiated HE, noise masking, series-based SMC, and
additive secret sharing, keeping client overhead to only 4–8% above a scheme without
any PPTs in place.

This guarantee is narrow, as it only covers semi-honest gradient inference under
a fixed threat model and a single fixed configuration. It does not address poten-
tial attacks such as gradient inversion [3], membership inference [4], and poisoning
attacks [5]. Zhang et al. [6] have proposed their No-Free-Lunch (NFL) theorem,
which states that privacy leakage, utility loss, and efficiency reduction cannot all
be minimised simultaneously, as attempting to reduce one the effects in one area
leads to increases in one or both of the other fields. A fixed configuration therefore
implicitly imposes a fixed tradeoff, with no mechanism for users to adjust it. Zhang
et al. also derived concrete lower bounds for four widely-used protection mecha-
nisms, randomisation, HE, secret sharing, and compression, providing a basis for
comparing and parameterising these techniques. For example, in Paillier HE, it is
found the utility loss is zero, but the privacy and efficiency trade off against the
cipher text sizen. Having a largernimproves privacy at the cost of communication
overhead.
This matters beyond just the three-way tradeoff: Chen et al. [7] show that pri-
vacy mechanisms can have cascading effects across other model properties, finding
that differential privacy disproportionately degrades accuracy for underrepresented
groups, and that fairness-aware training can in turn increase privacy risk. Together,
these results reinforce the case for a configurable framework that makes such trade-
offs explicit rather than burying them in a fixed design.
Aim
This thesis will expand on the scheme proposed by Lee et al. [2] and has two goals:
broadening security coverage to include gradient inversion, model inference attacks
and poisoning attacks, and developing a configurable privacy framework that lets
users tune the tradeoff between security, utility and overhead cost. The NFL bounds
of Zhang et al. [6] provide the theoretical foundation for mapping parameter choices
to security guarantees.
Research Questions
RQ1.How can Lee et al.’s scheme [2] be extended to cover gradient inversion, model
inference and poisoning attacks, and what are the resulting utility and efficiency
costs in light of the NFL tradeoffs [6]?
RQ2.Can the extended scheme be parameterised so that users can explicitly config-
ure the tradeoff between security coverage, adversary tolerance, and computational
overhead at a specified privacy level?
If both goals are achieved, this new framework would have broader applicability.
Real-world FL deployments rarely have uniform privacy needs. A hospital network,

for instance, may need stronger protections for sensitive patient records than for
anonymised research data, while a financial institution operating across different
countries may face different legal requirements in each jurisdiction. Rather than
building an entirely new system for each situation, the configurable framework de-
veloped in this project would allow users to simply dial up or down the level of
privacy protection to suit their specific context.
Method
Step 1: Threat analysis.HE, noise masking, secret sharing and chained routing
will be analysed against the threat models of gradient inversion [3], model infer-
ence [4] and poisoning [5]. For each threat, the analysis will ask whether the existing
scheme provides incidental protection, what minimal extension is needed if it does
not, and what the overhead cost of that extension is.
Step 2: Configurable parameters.The threat analysis will identify the param-
eters governing security level, expected to include: route length (n−2 adversary
tolerance), noise magnitude (gradient inversion resistance) and HE settings (encryp-
tion strength vs. speed). Each parameter will be formalised so that a target privacy
level maps to a concrete configuration.
Step 3: Implementation and evaluation.Lee et al.’s scheme [2] will be re-
produced using Python/PyTorch/TenSEAL as a baseline, then extended per Steps
1–2. Experiments across the MNIST, CIFAR-10, and SVHN datasets will measure
model accuracy, runtime overhead, and threat coverage as a function of privacy
configuration.
