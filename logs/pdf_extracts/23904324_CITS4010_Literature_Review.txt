1 Introduction
Machine learning has become increasingly important in privacy-sensitive domains
such as healthcare, finance, and public administration, yet its dependence on large
and representative datasets conflicts with legal, ethical, and organisational con-
straints on data sharing. For example, several hospitals may each hold clinically
valuable patient records, but cannot simply pool those records into a central dataset
because of privacy regulations and institutional data governance requirements. This
creates what Li et al. [1] describe as “data islands”: datasets that may be individu-
ally limited, but collectively valuable if they could be used for collaborative model
training.
Federated Learning (FL) addresses this problem by allowing multiple clients to train
a shared model without directly exchanging their raw data, formulated by Google
in 2019 [1]. In a typical FL process, a central server distributes a global model to
participating clients, each client trains the model locally on private data, and only
model updates, such as gradients or weight changes, are returned for aggregation.
For example, in a cross-hospital diagnosis task, each hospital could train locally on
its own patient records while contributing to a shared global model. In this way,
FL reduces the need for centralised data pooling while still allowing institutions to
benefit from distributed data.
However, FL’s privacy guarantee is conditional rather than intrinsic. Although raw
data remains local, the model updates shared during training can still leak sensitive
information. Gradient inversion attacks can reconstruct training inputs from gradi-
ents [2–4], membership inference attacks can determine whether a record was used
during training [5–8], source inference attacks can infer which client contributed
a record [5], and poisoning attacks can manipulate the global model by injecting
corrupted data or updates [9, 10]. These attacks show that data locality alone is
not sufficient: FL protects the location of data, but not necessarily the information
encoded in model updates or model behaviour.

To mitigate these risks, researchers have proposed several privacy-preserving and
robustness techniques. Homomorphic Encryption (HE) and secure aggregation can
hide plaintext gradients during aggregation [11, 12], Differential Privacy (DP) can
reduce membership leakage by adding calibrated noise [13], and robust aggregation
or recovery mechanisms can reduce the impact of poisoning attacks [14,15]. Yet each
defence protects only part of the attack surface. HE can protect gradients in transit,
but does not prevent inference from the final model’s behaviour. DP can reduce
membership leakage, but often reduces model accuracy. Robust aggregation can
defend model integrity, but does not provide confidentiality. The central challenge is
therefore not simply to add one privacy mechanism, but to understand how privacy,
utility, efficiency, and robustness can be balanced.
This review examines privacy-preserving FL through that lens. Section 2 introduces
FL and the cryptographic background needed to understand HE. Section 3 surveys
the main attack classes against FL and the threat models under which they oper-
ate. Section 4 reviews defence techniques, including HE-based secure aggregation,
DP, and poisoning defences. Section 5 examines the privacy–utility–efficiency trade-
off and explains why configurable privacy-preserving FL systems are needed. The
review concludes by identifying the research gap motivating this project: existing
HE-based FL schemes provide promising gradient confidentiality, but their tradeoffs
are often fixed, implicit, and evaluated under narrow threat models.
2 Background
2.1 Federated Learning
Federated Learning is a distributed machine learning paradigm in which multiple
clients collaboratively train a shared model while keeping their raw data local [1].
The most common training pattern is FedAvg. In each training round, the server
sends the current global model to a selected group of clients. Each client performs
local training on its private dataset and returns an update to the server. The server
then computes a weighted average of the received updates and uses it to produce
the next global model. Figure 1 summarises this process.

This process is attractive because it reduces the need to centralise sensitive data.

For example, in a hospital consortium, each hospital can train locally on its own
patient records while contributing to a shared diagnostic model. However, the server
still receives information derived from the local data. The privacy properties of FL
therefore depend on the type of data distribution, the communication architecture,
the scale of deployment, and the privacy mechanism used.
Li et al. [1] classify FL systems across dimensions including data distribution, model
type, privacy mechanism, communication architecture, federation scale, and deploy-
ment motivation. This taxonomy is important because attacks and defences do not
apply uniformly across all FL settings. For example, cross-device FL may involve
many unreliable mobile clients, while cross-silo FL usually involves fewer but more
stable institutional participants. A defence suitable for one setting may be impracti-
cal in the other. Similarly, horizontal FL, where clients share the same feature space
but hold different samples, creates different privacy risks from vertical FL, where
clients hold different features about overlapping users. This review focuses primarily
on horizontal FL because most of the attack and defence literature considered here
assumes shared model updates over similarly structured local datasets.
2.2 Homomorphic Encryption
Homomorphic Encryption (HE) is a cryptographic technique that allows compu-
tation to be performed directly on encrypted data. After decryption, the result
corresponds to the result that would have been obtained if the same operation had
been performed on the plaintext. In FL, this is useful because a server may be
able to aggregate encrypted client updates without seeing the individual plaintext
gradients.
This property makes HE a natural candidate for defending against attacks that re-
quire direct access to gradients. However, HE also introduces computational and
communication overhead, because encrypted values are larger and more expensive to
process than plaintext values. In machine learning, the CKKS scheme is especially
relevant because it supports approximate arithmetic over real numbers, which suits
neural network training [12]. The approximate nature of CKKS also introduces secu-
rity and correctness considerations: parameter choices such as polynomial modulus
degree, coefficient modulus, and multiplication depth affect the balance between
security, accuracy, and efficiency [16].
Therefore, HE should be understood as a powerful but configurable building block.
It can protect update confidentiality, but it does not automatically solve all FL
security problems. The detailed application of HE to FL is discussed in Section 4
as part of the defence landscape.
3 Attacks Against Federated Learning
Despite its goal of preserving data locality, FL is not inherently secure. The ex-
change of model updates creates several attack surfaces: an adversary may observe
gradients, query model predictions, track model behaviour across rounds, or manip-

ulate client updates. These attacks differ not only in method, but also in threat
model.
3.1 Threat Models in Federated Learning
An honest-but-curious, or passive, adversary follows the FL protocol but attempts
to infer private information from legitimately observed data such as gradients, aggre-
gated parameters, or model predictions. A typical example is a server that correctly
aggregates client updates but stores them for later analysis [17].
A colluding semi-honest adversary is stronger. In this setting, the server and some
clients cooperate to infer information about an honest client’s data. This threat
model is important for HE-based schemes, because secure aggregation must often
protect clients not only from the server, but also from combinations of server-client
collusion [11].
A malicious adversary deviates from the protocol. Rather than only observing in-
formation, it may submit poisoned data, manipulated gradients, or backdoored up-
dates. This model is central to poisoning attacks. Unlike privacy attacks, poisoning
attacks target model integrity: the adversary aims to degrade the model or force
specific misclassifications [9].
These threat models structure the remainder of this section. Gradient inversion and
membership inference usually focus on confidentiality, while poisoning focuses on
integrity. This distinction matters because a defence that hides gradients does not
necessarily detect malicious gradients.
3.2 Gradient Inversion Attacks
Gradient inversion attacks (GIAs) exploit the fact that the gradients a client trans-
mits encode information about the local training data used to produce them. An
adversary who observes a gradient vector can attempt to recover the original input
by solving an optimisation problem: initialise dummy inputs and labels, compute
their gradient, and iteratively adjust the dummy inputs until the resulting gradient
matches the observed one as closely as possible. For example, an honest-but-curious
server receiving a client’s plaintext image-classification gradient may attempt to
reconstruct the original image that generated that update.
Geiping et al. [2] established the severity of this threat by demonstrating that high-
resolution images can be faithfully reconstructed from gradients in realistic white-
box FL settings involving trained deep networks. Their cosine-similarity-based op-
timisation objective produces reconstructions that are visually close to the originals
in many cases. This result directly challenges the assumption that withholding
raw data is sufficient for privacy, because the gradient itself can act as a compact
representation of that data.
Geng et al. [3] broadened the attack to both FedSGD and FedAvg protocols and
improved label inference, showing that the threat is not confined to single-step gra-
dient descent. Guo et al. [4] provide a systematic taxonomy of GIA families such as

optimisation-based, generation-based, and analytics-based GIA, and evaluate their
practical viability under realistic deployment conditions, including larger batch sizes
and partial model access. Their analysis shows that optimisation-based attacks are
the most broadly practical, while generation-based attacks require auxiliary datasets
and analytics-based attacks are limited to specific architectures.
A critical contribution that affects the severity assessment comes from the eval-
uation methodology implicit in these works: attack success depends strongly on
assumptions about batch size, label availability, model architecture, and normal-
isation choices. Large batches reduce the information content per sample in the
gradient, making reconstruction harder; batch normalisation layers introduce sta-
tistical masking that also degrades reconstruction quality. This means GIAs are
neither universally devastating nor irrelevant: their practical impact is a function of
the specific FL configuration, and any claim about GIA resistance or vulnerability
must specify these conditions.
3.3 Model Inference Attacks
Model inference attacks extract information about the training set without recon-
structing raw inputs, instead operating on model outputs, parameter trajectories,
or behavioural patterns. Whereas GIAs ask whether the original data can be re-
covered, model inference asks whether private facts about participation, source, or
distribution can be inferred from the trained model or training history.
3.3.1 Membership Inference Attacks
Membership inference attacks (MIAs) ask whether a given data point was included
in the training set. Bai et al. [5] provide a comprehensive survey of MIAs in the
FL setting, organising attacks by adversary role (passive global, active global, mali-
cious local), observation channel (gradients, model parameters, predictions), and the
metric used to distinguish members from non-members. The common thread across
attack families is the exploitation of the model’s tendency to memorise training data,
which creates measurable statistical differences between the model’s behaviour on
members versus non-members. For example, if a hospital model behaves unusually
confidently on one patient’s record, an adversary may infer that the record was part
of the training set.
Liu et al. [6] exploit the temporal dimension of this phenomenon. Rather than
querying a static model, they track a target sample’s confidence trajectory across
training rounds, observing that membership status produces characteristic patterns
in how confidence evolves. Their TEAR attack achieves higher success rates than
single-round baselines, particularly under realistic federated configurations where
the global model is updated incrementally. He et al. [7] further extend MIAs by
aggregating confidence signals across multiple rounds and combining them with
data-poisoning techniques to improve attack accuracy while maintaining a minimal
footprint that is difficult to detect.
Zhu et al. [8] introduce FedMIA, which targets a federated configuration where

clients participate in multiple rounds and the adversary can observe updates over
time. They show that round-level differences contain membership signals that persist
even after aggregation, demonstrating that the temporal structure of FL training is
itself an information channel that static defences do not fully address.
The key implication for defence design is that MIAs do not necessarily require ac-
cess to plaintext gradients: they can operate on model behaviour and parameter
trajectories. Homomorphic encryption, which protects gradients in transit, does not
inherently reduce membership leakage from the final model.
3.3.2 Source Inference Attacks
Source inference attacks (SIAs) ask a stronger question than membership inference:
not just whether a record was in training, but which client it came from. This attack
class is particularly relevant in cross-silo FL, where revealing the source institution
of a training record can breach confidentiality even when the record itself is not
reconstructed. For example, if a record can be linked to a specific specialist clinic,
the source itself may reveal sensitive contextual information.
Bai et al. [5] situate source-oriented attacks within the broader MIA taxonomy,
noting that such inference exploits the heterogeneity of client datasets: because
different clients hold different data distributions, gradient or model-behaviour pat-
terns may carry client-identifiable information. An honest-but-curious server can
use these patterns, together with any available auxiliary information, to infer which
client contributed a given sample, especially when clients exhibit local overfitting.
This attack class reveals a gap between update confidentiality and participant con-
fidentiality. A scheme that encrypts gradients during aggregation may prevent the
server from reading individual updates in plaintext, but the final global model and
its behaviour on public data can still carry provenance signals. Source inference
therefore motivates defences, such as gradient perturbation or client-level differen-
tial privacy, that reduce the identifiability of individual clients’ contributions, not
just the readability of individual updates.
3.4 Poisoning Attacks
Poisoning attacks target the integrity of the global model rather than the confiden-
tiality of the training data. In FL, the distributed training process creates an inher-
ent vulnerability: the server aggregates updates from participating clients without
directly verifying the correctness of their local computations. A malicious client can
exploit this by submitting manipulated updates that shift the global model toward
a target objective.
Tolpegin et al. [9] provide a foundational analysis of data poisoning in FL, showing
that an adversary who controls even a small fraction of clients can substantially de-
grade global model accuracy by flipping or corrupting local labels. For example, if
malicious clients repeatedly relabel one image class as another during local training,
the global model may learn a targeted misclassification. Their experiments demon-
strate that the decentralised structure of FL can amplify adversarial inputs because

poisoned updates are averaged into the global model alongside benign updates.
Kasyap and Tripathy [10] extend the poisoning literature by proposing hyperdi-
mensional data poisoning attacks (HDPA). Rather than simply flipping labels, their
approach uses hyperdimensional computing to generate adversarial poisoned sam-
ples that preserve the apparent semantics of the original data while pushing the
model toward targeted misclassification. This makes the attack harder to detect
than simple label-flipping, because the poisoned samples are not necessarily obvious
outliers. Their results show that poisoning can remain effective even against some
Byzantine-robust defences, reinforcing the point that integrity protection requires
more than encrypted communication.
The central lesson for defence design is that poisoning is orthogonal to confiden-
tiality: encrypting gradients in transit does not prevent the server from faithfully
aggregating a poisoned update, because the poisoned update is still a valid cipher-
text whose plaintext is a malicious gradient. Protecting model integrity requires
mechanisms that can assess the quality or trustworthiness of updates, not merely
their secrecy.
3.5 Synthesis of the Attack Landscape
The attack literature shows that FL exposes information through multiple channels.
Gradient inversion is the most direct challenge to the assumption that updates are
safe to share, because it treats gradients as an encoded representation of the training
input. Membership and source inference are subtler: they may remain possible even
when gradients are hidden, because the global model can still memorise or reflect
patterns from particular clients. Poisoning is different again, because it does not
require learning private information at all; it exploits the server’s dependence on
client-supplied updates.
This comparison has two implications. First, attack severity cannot be assessed in
the abstract. A small-batch image classification task with plaintext gradients is a
very different setting from a cross-silo FL deployment using encrypted aggregation
and multiple local epochs. Second, no single defence should be described as making
FL “secure” unless the claim is limited to a specific observable and adversary. This
is why the remainder of the review separates confidentiality mechanisms, such as
HE and DP, from integrity mechanisms, such as robust aggregation and recovery.

model manipulation. The key organising principle for this section is that different
defences address different attacks: cryptographic approaches primarily counter gra-
dient inversion and honest-but-curious inference; statistical perturbation approaches
counter membership leakage; and integrity-focused mechanisms counter poisoning.

4.1 Homomorphic Encryption and Secure Aggregation
HE-based defences address gradient inversion and honest-but-curious server infer-
ence by preventing the server from observing plaintext gradients during aggrega-
tion. If the server only receives encrypted updates, it cannot directly apply an
optimisation-based reconstruction attack such as Geiping et al.’s gradient inversion
attack [2].
Lee et al. [11] propose a server-initiated HE scheme for FL that combines CKKS
encryption, noise masking, and additive secret sharing. The key design choice is
to shift expensive HE setup operations to the server, reducing the cryptographic
burden on resource-constrained clients. Their scheme is designed to protect against
colluding semi-honest participants and reports only 4–8% overhead with no observed
accuracy loss. This makes it an important practical baseline for HE-based FL.
FedSHE, proposed by Pan et al. [12], addresses a different practical limitation of
CKKS-based FL: the maximum vector length that can be encrypted in a single ci-
phertext. Large neural networks produce gradient vectors that exceed this limit,
so FedSHE introduces adaptive segmented encryption, splitting large gradients into
CKKS-compatible chunks before aggregation. Pan et al. also analyse how CKKS pa-
rameters affect correctness, security, and computational cost, showing that HE-based
FL requires careful parameter selection rather than default encryption settings.
These HE-based approaches are effective against attacks that require plaintext gra-

dients, especially gradient inversion by an honest-but-curious server. However, they
have two major limitations. First, HE protects updates during communication and
aggregation, but does not prevent inference from the final model’s behaviour. Mem-
bership inference attacks such as TEAR [6] and FedMIA [8] may still succeed because
they exploit prediction behaviour or parameter trajectories rather than raw plain-
text gradients. Second, HE does not prevent poisoning: an encrypted malicious
update is still malicious after decryption and aggregation. Therefore, HE should be
treated as a confidentiality layer, not a complete FL security solution.
4.2 Differential Privacy and Noise-Based Defences
Differential Privacy (DP) protects against membership inference and gradient-level
leakage by injecting calibrated noise into model updates before sharing, ensuring
that the contribution of any individual training record cannot be reliably inferred.
Wei et al. [13] provide a foundational analysis of FL with DP, deriving convergence
guarantees under Gaussian noise and showing that the privacy budgetεgoverns the
accuracy–privacy tradeoff: smallerε(stronger privacy) requires larger noise, which
degrades model convergence.
Against the membership inference attacks of Bai et al. [5] and He et al. [7], DP is the
most principled defence: by construction, a DP mechanism bounds the statistical
advantage any adversary can gain from observing the protected output. Against
source inference, client-level DP, where noise is added at the level of the entire client
update rather than individual records, reduces the gradient signatures that allow a
server to identify contributing clients.
The central limitation of DP-based defences is the accuracy–privacy tradeoff. Zhang
et al. [17] formalise this through the No-Free-Lunch theorem for FL, which shows
that no mechanism can simultaneously achieve minimal privacy leakage, minimal
utility loss, and minimal efficiency cost. For DP specifically, stronger privacy guar-
antees require larger noise budgets, which reduce the signal-to-noise ratio in the
aggregated gradient and can severely degrade model accuracy when the number of
local training samples is small or when the data is highly non-IID. This tradeoff is
examined in detail in the following section.
DP does not address every attack. A poisoned update that has been differentially
perturbed is still a poisoned update, so poisoning requires separate integrity de-
fences. Similarly, DP must be carefully composed with HE or secure aggregation if
both membership protection and encrypted aggregation are required.
4.3 Robust Aggregation and Recovery-Based Defences
Defending against poisoning requires mechanisms that assess the quality or trust-
worthiness of individual client updates, rather than their confidentiality. Panda et
al. [14] propose SparseFed, a defence that applies top-ksparsification to the aggre-
gated gradient: only theklargest entries are retained, under the assumption that
poisoned updates tend to affect a sparse, targeted set of parameters while benign
updates spread influence more broadly. By discarding the long tail of parameter

updates, SparseFed limits the impact of adversarial modifications while preserving
the signal from benign clients.
A complementary approach is recovery rather than prevention. Cao et al. [15] pro-
pose FedRecover, which stores historical model checkpoints and client update sum-
maries and uses them to reconstruct an uncontaminated model after a poisoning
attack has been detected. Rather than requiring real-time filtering of every update,
FedRecover spreads the defence cost by recovering post hoc from a trusted historical
state. Their experiments show that FedRecover can restore model performance to
near-baseline levels at lower computational cost than retraining from scratch.
Neither SparseFed nor FedRecover addresses gradient confidentiality. SparseFed op-
erates on aggregated gradients in the clear and would need to be composed with
an HE or secure aggregation scheme for deployments where the server is untrusted.
FedRecover requires the server to store and query historical updates, which raises ad-
ditional confidentiality concerns unless those records are themselves protected. The
composition of integrity defences with cryptographic defences is an active research
problem and a key motivation for the proposed configurable framework.
4.4 Combining Defence Layers
A practical FL deployment is unlikely to rely on only one defence. A hospital
consortium, for example, may need HE to prevent the coordinating server from
seeing gradients, DP to reduce membership leakage from the trained model, and
robust aggregation to limit the influence of compromised participants. However,
combining defences is not additive in a simple way. HE may prevent the server from
inspecting individual updates, but that same opacity can make anomaly detection
harder. DP may reduce membership leakage, but the added noise can also make
benign and malicious updates more difficult to distinguish. Recovery methods such
as FedRecover require historical state, but storing historical updates may increase
the amount of sensitive material that must be protected.
This creates a system-level design problem. The designer must decide not only
which mechanisms to include, but also where they sit in the FL pipeline and which
information each mechanism is allowed to observe. A robust aggregation rule that
requires plaintext updates may be incompatible with a strict HE design unless the
aggregation is redesigned to work over ciphertexts or trusted execution is introduced.
Conversely, a fully encrypted aggregation pipeline may achieve confidentiality while
leaving the system blind to poisoned updates. These tensions support the central
argument of this review: privacy-preserving FL should expose defence choices and
their costs explicitly, rather than presenting a fixed protocol as universally appro-
priate.

5 Privacy–Utility–Efficiency Tradeoffs in Feder-
ated Learning
A recurring theme across all privacy-preserving techniques is the presence of fun-
damental tradeoffs between privacy protection, model utility, and computational
efficiency. This section examines that tradeoff structure formally, drawing on the
No-Free-Lunch theorem and multi-objective optimisation frameworks.

may reduce model accuracy. Homomorphic encryption can preserve model accu-
racy by encrypting updates without changing their plaintext values, but it increases
computation and communication cost.
The importance of Zhang et al.’s theorem for this review is that it changes the
evaluation question. Instead of asking which defence is universally best, the relevant
question becomes: which operating point is suitable for a particular deployment? A
hospital network may accept highε e if stronger privacy is required, while a resource-
constrained edge deployment may tolerate weaker privacy to reduce communication
overhead. For the proposed research, this supports the need for configurable HE-

For randomisation mechanisms, stronger privacy is achieved by increasing the noise
scale, but this also increases utility loss because the aggregated gradients contain
less useful training signal. The NFL result therefore transforms the discussion from
“which mechanism is best?” to “which operating point on the tradeoff frontier is
appropriate for a given deployment?” This reframing has direct implications for
system design: a fixed configuration implicitly selects one point on the frontier,
offering no mechanism for users to adjust protection strength when the deployment
context changes.
5.2 Multi-Objective Optimisation and Pareto Fronts
Kang et al. [18] operationalise the NFL tradeoff by framing FL as a constrained
multi-objective optimisation problem (CMOFL). Their formulation jointly optimises
privacy leakage, utility loss, and training cost, subject to upper bounds on each
objective, and searches for Pareto-optimal configurations, where no objective can
be improved without worsening another. They propose two algorithms, CMOFL-
NSGA-II (an evolutionary approach) and CMOFL-PSL (a Bayesian optimisation
approach), and evaluate them across privacy mechanisms including randomisation,
BatchCrypt (HE-based), and sparsification on Fashion-MNIST and CIFAR-10.
Their experimental results confirm that the Pareto front is non-trivial: there is
a rich set of configurations that represent different acceptable balances between
privacy, utility, and efficiency, and the best configuration depends on domain-specific
priorities. A hospital network may be willing to accept higher computation cost
in exchange for stronger privacy guarantees; a mobile application may prioritise
efficiency and tolerate a weaker privacy budget. The CMOFL framework makes
this choice explicit rather than embedding it in fixed protocol parameters.
5.3 Privacy, Fairness, and Cascading Effects
Chen et al. [19] identify an additional dimension of the tradeoff that is often over-
looked: the interaction between privacy mechanisms and model fairness. Their
analysis shows that differential privacy can disproportionately degrade accuracy for
underrepresented groups, because the noise added to obscure individual contribu-
tions may also mask the signal from low-frequency patterns in the data. Conversely,
fairness-aware training objectives that up-weight minority-group examples can in-
crease privacy risk by amplifying the contribution of individual records. These cas-
cading effects mean that the privacy–utility frontier in practice is higher-dimensional
than the three-objective formulation of Zhang et al. [17]: choices that appear Pareto-
optimal under privacy, accuracy, and efficiency objectives may still impose hidden
costs on fairness or equity.
For a configurable privacy framework, this finding motivates exposing fairness as an
additional consideration when selecting operating points, particularly in cross-silo

deployments where clients may represent demographically heterogeneous popula-
tions.
5.4 Implications for System Design
Together, the NFL theorem, the CMOFL framework, and the fairness analysis mo-
tivate a clear design principle: privacy mechanisms should be configurable rather
than fixed, and their configurations should be mapped to measurable guarantees
and costs so that users can make informed choices. Current HE-based frameworks,
including the server-initiated scheme of Lee et al. [11] and FedSHE [12], embed spe-
cific CKKS parameter choices that determine where the system sits on the privacy–
utility–efficiency frontier. Those choices are currently opaque to the end user. Ex-
posing them as explicit control surfaces, along with estimates of the corresponding
privacy leakage, accuracy impact, and overhead, would allow deployments with dif-
ferent priorities to select the configuration most appropriate for their context.
6 Conclusion
This review has examined the state of privacy-preserving federated learning across
four dimensions: the architectural and cryptographic foundations of FL; the at-
tack landscape; the defence landscape; and the fundamental tradeoff structure that
constrains any privacy-preserving design.
The foundational survey of Li et al. [1] establishes that FL systems span a wide range
of architectures and threat models, and that the privacy properties of any mechanism
depend critically on the specific configuration. The HE literature, exemplified by
Lee et al. [11] and FedSHE [12], demonstrates that cryptographic protection of
gradients in transit is achievable at practical overhead. The security analysis of Li
and Micciancio [16] adds the important caveat that the approximate arithmetic of
CKKS requires careful parameter choices to achieve rigorous guarantees.
The attack literature reveals that the FL privacy threat is multi-channel and multi-
class. Gradient inversion attacks [2–4] show that shared gradients can reconstruct
training inputs under realistic conditions. Membership inference attacks [5–8] demon-
strate that model behaviour reveals training set membership even when gradients are
protected. Poisoning attacks [9, 10] show that model integrity can be compromised
independently of gradient confidentiality.
The defence literature responds with mechanisms that are effective within their
respective scope but incomplete across the full attack surface. HE and secure ag-
gregation protect gradients in transit against honest-but-curious and colluding ad-
versaries [11, 12], but do not prevent inference from model behaviour. Differential
privacy reduces membership leakage but at the cost of utility [13]. Robust ag-
gregation and recovery mechanisms address poisoning [14, 15], but do not address
confidentiality. No single mechanism covers all three attack classes simultaneously.
The tradeoff literature formalises why this is not a matter of insufficient engineer-
ing effort but a structural property of the problem. The NFL theorem of Zhang et

al. [17] establishes that privacy, utility, and efficiency cannot all be simultaneously
minimised. The multi-objective framework of Kang et al. [18] shows that the appro-
priate response is not to search for a universally best mechanism, but to make the
tradeoff frontier explicit and allow users to select operating points that match their
deployment priorities. Chen et al. [19] extend this picture by showing that fairness
is an additional dimension of the tradeoff that fixed configurations can obscure.
The research gap identified by this review is therefore fourfold. Current defences are
attack-specific, leaving FL systems vulnerable to the classes of attack they were not
designed to address. Existing HE-based frameworks embed their privacy–utility–
efficiency tradeoffs in fixed protocol parameters rather than exposing them as user-
configurable choices. Most mechanisms are evaluated under narrow threat models
that do not reflect the full multi-class attack landscape. And the theory of multi-
objective optimisation, which provides the tools needed to navigate the tradeoff
frontier explicitly, has not yet been tightly coupled to practical system implementa-
tions. Addressing these gaps by extending a practical HE-based baseline to broader
attack coverage and exposing its tradeoff structure as an explicit configuration sur-
face forms the central objective of the proposed research.
